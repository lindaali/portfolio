— INTRODUCTION —

In my Innovated Product Design class, I was paired with five students with diverse backgrounds and industry experience to develop a new product. We explored the human-centered design approch and followed the product development cycle in the context of both a startup and established firm. Our process was reviewed throughout the semester with multiple design presentations and a final showcase with industry advisors from Amazon, Apple, Google, and others.

— CHALLENGE —

Redesign the senior experience to be so game-changing that it sets a new standard for the elderly care industry.

— VITATECH —

MISSION

Recognizing the challenges of seniors, we provide longevity solutions for all. We are dedicated to enriching lives and promoting independence.

GOALS

Accounting for 13% of the population, the elderly care sector has one of the largest expenditures of any industry. We intend to stay ahead of this growing industry to identify and provide solutions to issues before they become more widescale.

PRODUCT

Our first product is the uCan, a no-lift trash that increases the ease of independent living to anyone with mobility concerns. The uCan allows users to push a foot pepdal in order to lift their garbage out of the garbage can, thereby reducing strain on their back muscles and upper body joints.

— TIMELINE —

This project was developed over the course of one academic semester. The first half of the semester was dedicated to empathy fieldwork and ideation while protoyping, manufacturing, and testing were reserved for the second half.

— EMPATHY FIELDWORK —

To design a human-centered product, we needed to understand the people we were designing for. For this, we conducted extensive empathy fieldwork where we observed, engaged, and immersed ourselves in user context experiences. We conducted 21 episodes with users and caretakers in 8 different environments, cummulating to over 200 emotional data points.

— DEFINE —

UNPACKING

After collecting our empathy fieldwork, we began unpacking and analyzing the data points in terms of facts, emotions, insights, needs, and surprises. We also connected our notes and emotions per episode with patterns of tensions, contradictions, consistencies, synergies, and hmmm moments. This helped us frame the problem and guide our ideas. We then took each data point and categorized them into 5 overarching groups. Subgroups were then created within each general group and relationships were found again. Our main categories of user needs were impairments (30%), enrichment (30%), emotional peace (21%), necessities (14%), and caregivers (10%).

FLOW OF THOUGHTS

The elderly struggle with many impairments, primarily physical. These difficulties preclude them from participating in the things that make them feel fulfilled, resulting in discontent and loss of hope. If they had a way to manage or overcome impairments, they could be more independent, which would allow them to feel more enriched, accomplish basic needs, and relieve burden from caregivers.

PERSONAS

In order to better understand our users, we created personas to represent the variety of users our product would need to cater to.

dorris

Dorris is an aging grandmother and lives independently in a retirement community. She is very social, but, is beginning to lose her mobility. She feels trapped as her grandchildren begin to grow up and move away and is worried about having to move out of independent living.

harold

Harold is a recent retiree and lives alone at home after the passing of his wife. He enjoys outdoor activities and feeling physically healthy. He worries about maintaining his house and staying active as he grows older and is adjusting to life at home alone.

craig

Craig is a registered nurse working in an assisted living facility. He often gets frustrated by the lack of resources at the facility because he finds that, while he can provide quality care to those with more urgent medical needs, he does not have time to help people without those medical needs have a decent quality of life.

— IDEATE —

At this point, it was time to start generating ideas. We had three 45 minute brainstorming sessions where we wrote down every possible idea or solution we could think of. From this, we came up with over 100 different products including a modular walker, a device to help zip up clothing, and even an outdoor walking conveyor belt system. We then took each idea and grouped them into categories of similar products and rated them based on their financial and technical feasibility versus user desirablity. Based on feedback from our industry advisors and our data analysis, we moved forward with the no-lift trashcan.

— PROTOTYPE & TEST —

DESIGN 0

Our first prototype was very rudimentary and essentially built out of scraps. We used cardboard to create a container and platform. Chopsticks and fishing line were used as the lifting mechanism to raise a tiny bag of trash. This would not be our final mechanism. We had multiple ideas in mind for the lift including scissor lifts, hand cranks, pulleys, and a rack and pinion, however, the main function of this first protoype was to gather feedback on the concept overall.

testing fieldwork

We then took our first prototype to the streets. We talked to friends, family, and senior citizens in different environments and had them interact with our prototype. We received a lot of helpful feedback such as that most people preferred a foot pedal to a hand crank/lever. We also talked to many people who were satisfied with their current situation, but, we believe that many of these people had not thought about improvements to their current trash situation. A lot of people also commented on the usefulness of our product for seniors with back problems or other limitations without even knowing that the product's intended audience was senior citizens.

conjoint analysis

In addition to the testing fieldwork, we also created a conjoint analysis survey that was posted publicly. This survey asked users to rank the value of different attributes such as cost and mechanism. It then took those attributes and created numerous combinations of them all and asked users to rank the combinations overall. From this we discovered that the average consumer places more importance on the aesthetics and cost of the product, rather than the technical elements that we initially deemed most valuable.

DESIGN 1

iteration

We took the feedback from testing and the survey results and related it back to our initial goals. What we came up with was that this product would change the game for individuals with back issues, that it would need to function well and fit in nicely with the rest of the home in order for people to want it, and that some different disabilities would require different solutions.

This was the point where we really began refining our lifting mechanism. We decided to use a scissor lift to raise the platform and a pneumatic cylinder to convert the oscillatory pedal motion to a linear motion. We used two of these scissor lifts on either side of the platform for added stability which are connected by a stationary bar propelled by the piston. The piston operates using the compressed air created from the foot pump. We used a single acting cylinder to take advantage of the internal springs to retract the piston when we want the platform to lower. In addition, we integrated track rails and rollers for the scissor lifts to prevent binding and to achieve a smooth motion.
